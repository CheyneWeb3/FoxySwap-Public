DeFi Order Types on EVM — Market, Limit & Stop

How FoxySwap approaches execution on decentralised EVM chains (AMMs, intents, keepers, and risk controls).

The Core DeFi Idea

On decentralised EVM blockchains, orders are smart-contract interactions. You balance speed, price control, and automation while paying gas and facing on-chain conditions (mempool, MEV, liquidity depth). FoxySwap aligns these into three familiar modes: Market, Limit, and Stop orders.

Quick Definitions (DeFi Context)

  • Market Swap: Execute now via AMMs/aggregators at the best on-chain quotes within your slippage tolerance.
  • Limit Order: Execute only at your target price or better, typically via off-chain order books/intents or on-chain keeper execution (no fill until price meets conditions).
  • Stop Order: Trigger at a specific price to exit risk (stop-loss) or enter momentum (breakout). Implemented via keeper bots or intents → on-chain execution.

Concrete EVM Examples

1) Market Swap (AMM)

Swap TOKEN → WETH now. You approve once (or use Permit2), set slippage (e.g., 0.5%), submit the transaction, and pay gas. Pro: instant Con: slippage/MEV risk

2) Limit Order (Keeper/Intent)

You want WETH at a cheaper price. Place a limit order at your target. A keeper (or solver) only executes on-chain when the price meets your level within your tolerances. No price match → no fill → no swap.

3) Stop Order (Protective Exit)

You’re long TOKEN from 1.00 and will bail at 0.92. A stop-market exits quickly at/through 0.92 (may slip); a stop-limit exits only at 0.92 or better (safer price, but can miss in fast drops).

4) Stop Order (Breakout Entry)

Consolidation top is 1.20. Place a buy stop at 1.21 to enter if momentum confirms. The keeper submits your txn once the on-chain oracle/quote crosses your trigger.

Note: In DeFi, “stop” is the trigger. Execution can be stop-market (fast, can slip) or stop-limit (price-controlled, can miss).

Choosing the Right Tool on FoxySwap

  • Use Market for time-sensitive swaps and simple rebalances.
  • Use Limit when you care more about price than immediacy.
  • Use Stop to automate exits (risk caps) or breakout entries.

DeFi-Specific Pitfalls (and Fixes)

  • High price impact: Pools are thin. Fix: split orders; let aggregator route across pools.
  • Slippage reverts: Slippage too tight. Fix: widen tolerance slightly or trade during peak liquidity.
  • MEV sandwiching: Public mempool risk. Fix: use private relays/MEV-protection if available.
  • Allowance gotchas: Missing or too low allowance. Fix: approve exact or use Permit2; monitor spenders.
  • Gas spikes: Network congestion. Fix: set max fee caps; avoid volatile windows; batch actions when possible.

Mini-Glossary (EVM)

Slippage Allowed % difference between quote and execution to avoid failed swaps.

Price Impact Change caused by your trade size vs pool depth.

Allowance ERC-20 approval letting a contract spend your tokens.

Permit2 Gas-efficient delegated approvals (signature-based).

MEV Miner/validator extractable value; can include sandwiches and reorders.

Pro Setup: For large orders, consider TWAP (time-weighted) execution, private orderflow, or staged limits to reduce impact and MEV exposure.

FAQ

Why did my market swap slip?

Liquidity was thin or price moved between quote and inclusion. Increase liquidity sources or adjust slippage.

Do limits/Stops guarantee fills?

Limits guarantee price (or better), not execution. Stops are triggers; stop-market usually fills (may slip), stop-limit may miss.

What about approvals?

ERC-20 tokens require an approval for the router/executor. Revoke or reduce allowances you no longer need.

Tip: Pair a limit entry with a stop-loss and a take-profit limit for a fully defined on-chain trade.
